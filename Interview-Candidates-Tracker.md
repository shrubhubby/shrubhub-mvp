## Where to Find Candidates

### Personal Network (Start here - easiest)
- [ ] Friends who garden
- [ ] Family members
- [ ] Coworkers
- [ ] Neighbors you've seen gardening
- [ ] Friends-of-friends (ask for intros)

**Action:** Make a list of specific people you know

### Local Community
- [ ] Community gardens in your area
  - Visit during weekend mornings (busiest time)
  - Bring flyers or just chat with people
- [ ] Gardening clubs/societies
  - Search "[Your City] gardening club"
  - Attend a meeting and ask for volunteers
- [ ] Farmers markets
  - Talk to vendors who grow plants
  - Chat with customers buying plants
- [ ] Local nurseries
  - Ask staff if they'd be willing to chat
  - Post a flyer on community board
- [ ] Nextdoor or neighborhood apps
  - Post: "Looking for gardeners to chat with about plant care!"

## Tips for Finding Diverse Candidates

**Diversity to aim for:**

**Experience levels:**
- Beginner (started during pandemic, still learning)
- Intermediate (few years, feels competent)
- Advanced (decades of experience, deep knowledge)

**Garden types:**
- Indoor only (houseplants)
- Outdoor only (yard, vegetable garden)
- Mix of both
- Container/patio gardening
- Community garden plot

**Tech savviness:**
- Very tech-forward (uses multiple apps)
- Moderate (comfortable but not enthusiastic)
- Tech-averse (prefers analog)

**Demographics:**
- Different age ranges
- Urban vs. suburban
- Apartment vs. house

**Why diversity matters:** Different users will have different needs. A 60-year-old with a backyard vegetable garden has different problems than a 25-year-old with Instagram-worthy houseplants.

## Questions to Ask When Recruiting

To quickly assess if they're a good fit:

1. "How long have you been gardening?"
2. "What do you grow? Indoor or outdoor?"
3. "Have you ever used any apps or tools for gardening?"

This helps you ensure you're getting a diverse mix.
