# ShrubHub User Interview Guide

## Goals of These Interviews

Before building ShrubHub, we need to validate key assumptions:

1. **Do people struggle with plant care tracking?** (Problem validation)
2. **How do they currently solve this?** (Existing solutions/workarounds)
3. **Would conversational AI feel natural or weird?** (Core UX hypothesis)
4. **What would motivate daily engagement?** (Retention driver)
5. **What features matter most?** (Priority validation)

## Who to Interview

**Target: 5-10 people who:**
- Actively care for plants (indoor or outdoor)
- Range from beginners to experienced gardeners
- Mix of ages and tech comfort levels
- At least a few who've tried gardening apps before

**Where to find them:**
- Friends/family who garden
- Local gardening clubs or community gardens
- Nextdoor or neighborhood groups
- Plant/gardening subreddits (r/gardening, r/houseplants)
- Instagram gardening community
- Farmers markets (talk to vendors)

## Interview Best Practices

### The Mom Test Principles

**Don't ask:** "Would you use an app that helps you track your plants?"
**Why not:** People lie to be polite. Future hypotheticals aren't reliable.

**Do ask:** "Tell me about the last time you forgot to water a plant" or "Walk me through how you decide what to do in your garden each day"
**Why:** Past behavior reveals truth. Stories show real problems.

### Golden Rules

1. **Talk about their life, not your idea** - Learn about their world first
2. **Ask about specifics in the past** - "Last time you..." not "Do you usually..."
3. **Listen more than talk** - 80/20 rule
4. **Take notes** - Ask permission to record, or write notes
5. **Dig deeper** - Ask "why" and "tell me more about that"
6. **Don't pitch your solution** - You're learning, not selling

### Interview Structure (30-45 minutes)

**1. Warm-up (5 min)**
- Build rapport
- Explain you're researching plant care, not selling anything
- Ask permission to take notes

**2. Current behavior (15 min)**
- How they garden now
- Pain points and frustrations
- Current tools/methods

**3. Specific scenarios (15 min)**
- Walk through recent experiences
- Dig into problems they mentioned

**4. Aspirations (5 min)**
- How they wish it could be
- What would make them excited

**5. Concept reaction (5 min - OPTIONAL)**
- Only if conversation has gone well
- Show simple concept, observe reaction
- Don't defend, just learn

## Interview Questions

### Section 1: Gardening Background (Warm-up)

**Goal:** Understand their experience level and context

- Tell me about your garden(s). What do you grow?
- How long have you been gardening?
- What got you into gardening?
- What do you love most about it? What's most frustrating?

### Section 2: Current Plant Care Practices

**Goal:** Understand existing behavior and tools

- Walk me through a typical week in your garden. What do you actually do?
- How do you decide what needs attention on any given day?
- Do you track anything about your plants? (notes, photos, schedule)
  - *If yes:* Show me how you do that. What system do you use?
  - *If no:* Why not? Have you tried before?
- Tell me about a time you forgot something important (watering, fertilizing, etc.). What happened?
- How do you remember when you planted things or where specific plants are?
- Have you ever tried using an app or tool for gardening?
  - *If yes:* Which ones? What did you like/dislike? Do you still use it? Why or why not?
  - *If no:* Why not? What's held you back?

### Section 3: Plant Identification & Learning

**Goal:** Validate plant ID and knowledge features

- Tell me about the last time you didn't know what a plant was or what was wrong with it. How did you figure it out?
- When you want to learn about a plant's care needs, where do you go?
- How do you feel about your knowledge level? Confident or constantly Googling?
- Have you ever used a plant identification app? (like PlantNet, PictureThis, etc.)
  - *If yes:* When was the last time? What was that experience like?

### Section 4: Record Keeping & Memory

**Goal:** Understand pain points around tracking and memory

- Describe a time when you wish you had written something down about your plants.
- Do you take photos of your plants? What for?
- If you have multiple plants of the same type, how do you tell them apart or remember which is which?
- Tell me about trying to remember when you last watered or fertilized something.
- Have you ever tried to grow something from seed or propagate? How did you track that?

### Section 5: Relationship With Plants

**Goal:** Validate the "plants as beings" philosophy

- Do you name your plants?
- Do you talk to your plants?
- How do you feel when a plant dies?
- Tell me about a plant you're particularly attached to. What makes it special?
- Some people think of plants as decorations, others as pets or companions. Where do you land?

### Section 6: Technology Comfort & Habits

**Goal:** Understand tech usage and AI comfort

- What apps do you use daily? What keeps you coming back?
- Do you ever use voice assistants? (Siri, Alexa, Google) For what?
- Have you used any AI tools recently? (ChatGPT, etc.) What was that like?
- When you're out in the garden, do you usually have your phone with you?
- Would you rather type notes or speak them? Why?

### Section 7: Social & Community Aspects

**Goal:** Validate social features priority

- Do you trade plants or cuttings with friends/neighbors?
- Are you part of any gardening communities? (online, IRL, both)
- Tell me about the last time you got a plant from someone or gave one away.
- Do you ever share photos of your garden? Where? Why?

### Section 8: Scenario Deep-Dives

**Goal:** Get specific stories that reveal real behavior

Pick 2-3 based on what they've mentioned:

- **If they mentioned forgetting:** "Tell me more about that time you forgot to water. Walk me through what happened, how you discovered it, what you did."

- **If they use an app:** "Show me the app. What's the last thing you logged? When was that? Why did you log that specific thing?"

- **If they don't use an app:** "Tell me about a time you tried to remember when you planted something. What was that like?"

- **If they propagate:** "Walk me through the last time you propagated a plant. How did you track whether it worked?"

- **If they mentioned plant death:** "Tell me about losing that plant. What happened? Did you know what went wrong?"

### Section 9: Aspirational (Future State)

**Goal:** Understand what would delight them

- If you had a magic wand and could change one thing about plant care, what would it be?
- What would make you excited to check a gardening app every day?
- Imagine you're bragging to a friend about how well your garden is doing. What would you be saying?

### Section 10: Concept Reaction (OPTIONAL - Only if time and rapport)

**Setup:**
"I've been thinking about a concept and I'm curious about your gut reaction. This is super early and might be terrible, so be brutally honest."

**Show:** Simple wireframe or describe:
"An app where you can photograph your plants, and then just chat with an AI about them - like texting a knowledgeable gardener friend. You could say 'my tomato leaves are yellowing' and it would help troubleshoot, or ask it to remind you to water your basil."

**Ask:**
- What's your immediate reaction?
- *If positive:* What appeals to you about that?
- *If negative:* What feels off?
- Would you rather chat with AI or tap through menus/forms? Why?
- Does talking to AI about plants feel weird or natural?

**IMPORTANT:** Don't defend or explain. Just observe and ask why.

## What to Listen For

### Green Flags (Validate)
- "I constantly forget when I planted things"
- "I have notes scattered everywhere - notebook, phone notes, paper tags"
- "I wish I could just ask someone what's wrong"
- "I take tons of plant photos but never organize them"
- "I've tried apps but data entry is tedious"
- "I talk to my plants already" (validates philosophy)
- Shows you their current messy system unprompted

### Red Flags (Challenge assumptions)
- "I don't really track anything and that's fine"
- "I prefer figuring things out myself" (might not want AI guidance)
- "I hate talking to bots, they never understand"
- "I barely use my phone when gardening"
- "Apps make it feel too clinical/scientific"
- Can't think of any pain points (maybe problem isn't real for them)

### Insights to Capture
- Specific emotional moments (frustration, delight, loss)
- Workarounds they've created (reveals real problems)
- Apps they abandoned (why?)
- Words and phrases they use (for copywriting)
- Unexpected use cases you hadn't considered

## After 5-10 Interviews: Analysis

### Look for Patterns

**Common pain points:**
- List problems mentioned by 3+ people
- These are your high-priority features

**Polarizing reactions:**
- If AI chat is 50/50 loved/hated, that's a decision point
- Might need to offer both chat and traditional UI

**Unexpected insights:**
- Features you didn't think of
- Use cases you missed
- Different user segments (beginners vs. experts need different things)

### Questions to Answer

1. **Is the problem real?** Do people actually struggle with plant tracking?
2. **Is your solution desirable?** Do they respond positively to conversational AI?
3. **Will they use it?** Can you identify a clear trigger for when they'd open the app?
4. **What's the MVP?** What single feature would provide immediate value?

### Decision Framework

**If most people say:**
- "I love the idea of chatting with AI about my plants" → Build conversational MVP as planned
- "AI feels weird, I'd rather have a simple form" → Reconsider UX approach
- "I don't really track anything now" → Need to create new behavior (harder) or find better target users
- "Plant ID is the most valuable part" → Maybe start with identification tool, add chat later

## Next Steps After Interviews

1. **Synthesize findings** - Create summary document of patterns
2. **Adjust MVP scope** - Based on what actually matters to users
3. **Prioritize features** - Reorder based on pain points discovered
4. **Refine messaging** - Use language they used
5. **Identify early beta testers** - Ask if they'd try it when ready
